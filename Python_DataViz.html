1: Visualization Exercise¶

Here, three alternative visualizations of the same artificial data shall be created by you. All plots display fictitious genomic annotations together with fictitious RNA binding protein data. The visualizations shall display RNA binding protein signals and link these binding information to genomic annotations to shed light on potential biological function. For each version a reference is shown. Two different datasets are provided:

  • 10_project_data_annotations.csv
  • 10_project_data_signals.csv

The 10_project_data_annotations.csv file contains fictitious genomic information as visualized in all bottom panels of the example plots. Each horizontal line represents a transcript. A transcript can contain multiple exons (grey rectangles). Transcripts can be located on the '+' or on the '-' strand of the DNA.

10_project_data_signals.csv contains fictitious signals of four RNA binding proteins (P1, P2, P3, P4).

1.4: Discussion¶

Discuss the pros and cons between the different visualization approaches.

Version 1:

Pros:

  • Offers a significant amount of details.
  • Can be used to find specific data points, e.g., 'at genomic position 10000, P4 shows a dip within a peak'.

Cons:

  • Extended visualization is difficult to see at once for the viewer.
  • Separate subplots may offer too much information at once

Version 2:

Pros:

  • Compacted view offers similar trend-line information as version 1.
  • Easier for the viewer to compare the 4 samples.

Cons:

  • Much less detail offered than version 1
  • Easy to miss subtleties if this is the only plot used to derive insights.

Version 3:

Pros:

  • Offers the benefits of both previous versions: trends are visualized without being overly expansive or compact

Cons:

  • Information in this plot may be somewhat cluttered and difficult to read, as the data from different samples are overlaid on one another.

A comment on the use of these visualizations: Genome browsers offer many styles of visualization, because each individual one will have drawbacks. Being able to view each visualization as required is crucial to get all the necessary data. In the case of scientific publications, compact/summarized data is handy when explaining results, but expansive visualizations may be much more helpful in the appendix, for future researchers to rely on.

2: Text Exercise¶

Summarize the article 'Pencil and paper' by Wong & Kjaergaard. What are the key points? Do not copy and paste from the article. Summarize in your own words.

The article 'Pen and paper' (Wong & Kjaergaard, 2012) extolls the benefits of physical drawing as a first step in scientific visualization. The authors' premise starts by making a distinction between the skills needed to verbalize a concept (e.g., the location of a protein) and visualizing that same concept. They note that visual representation forces us to contextualize the concept in terms of space (an idea that may be extended to dimensions such as size, color and orientation). In their example, they note that 'intracellular' is not sufficient to visually describe a protein - the representation of the protein within the cell must be assigned either nuclear, cytoplasmic or membrane-associated spaces, which forces us to also represent those demarcations within the cell.

The authors then specify that the pen-and-paper method should not create the end result, but act as a starting point. This is due to the fact that visualizing with pen on paper (or chalk on board, digital pen on tablet, with a finger in the dirt...) allows us to quickly articulate our first idea without the roadblock of knowing how to create this. If we were to start out with a drawing programme, then there is a chance of 'losing' the idea while trying to understand how to use the programme.

The authors argue that drawing also improves comprehension (which may, in turn, lead to better visualizations). Furthermore, it circumvents the limited working memory of the average human brain. Remembering a series of numbers is difficult, but remembering a trend line is easier. Remembering a set of directions is difficult, but reading a map is easier. The authors argue that drawing, therefore, is a good way to extend our working memory. Arguably, the same can be said about just making notes, but their point stands.

Their arguments are solidified by the examples given, wherein we see the progression of R. Vale and R. Milligan's work from handmade sketches to an animated model of kinesin traveling along microtubules. It highlights the fact that an easy-to-read visualization often needs a lot of work on the creator's side. This work begins with simply articu;ating in the most primitive form possible, the base idea. These sketches can then be built upon for the final result.

I would add that the article hints (but does not say explicitly) that these first visualizations also encourage us to consider how the information will be viewed by others. Drawing out the idea is also likely to bring up further questions that need to be answered. Both of these points improve the potential for communicating the idea more effectively.
